DAND Project WeRateDogs: Ratings improve over time and Tweets are more likely on weekdays¶

Introduction

WeRateDogs is a Twitter account that rates people's dogs with a humorous comment about the dog. The Twitter account was opened in 2015 and quickly became popular.

One reason for the popularity is the unique rating system. The dogs often receive scores above 10/10. The discussion about the rating system received a lot of attention. The quote from the guy behind @dog_rates defending the rating system "They're Good Dogs Brent" is even sold on T-Shirts (https://knowyourmeme.com/memes/theyre-good-dogs-brent and https://weratedogs.com).

We collect over 2 thousand Tweets from WeRateDogs to study the phenomenon more closely.

The rating system over time¶

The rating is not always an 'out of 10'. The numbers go higher when there are multiple dogs on a single picture. However, if we ignore rare occasions we have 'out of 10' and we can tackle the question do the ratings have a time trend?

We take the data from 2015 to 2017. After some tedious work to extract the ratings from the Tweets, we can review the average rating numerator over time. The following graph shows that there is considerable inflation of the ratings given. The average rating from 2015 is 9.3 compared to 10.4 for 2017.

Tweet days¶

If you're a WeRateDogs fan and you're waiting for the next tweet, you might ask on which day can I expect most Tweets? You can expect about 3 Tweets per day, excluding retweets and later removed Tweets. In general, on weekdays you can expect 19\% more Tweets. Among the weekdays Monday is the WeRateDogs most active day.

Tweet popularity by algorithm ability to distinguish the picture¶

When an algorithm identifies a dog on the picture the dog is visible on the picture in a certain structure. Does that structure correlate with how humans like the picture? Here as a first approach, we measure how much the picture is liked by the retweet count. We separate the dog pictures into two groups. The first group contains the pictures where the applied neural network decides that it is a dog on the picture. The second group contains the remaining pictures.

If the algorithm recognizes a dog this might mean the dog is rather clear on the picture. Rather vaguely, a recognized picture will have a certain structure compared to not recognized pictures.

The graph shows two histograms. One for dogs recognized, one for dogs not recognized. Both distributions are right-skewed and rather similar. This means there is visually no clear correlation between the number of retweets and algorithm classification.

The pure numbers show that pictures with dogs identified have a 4\% higher mean retweet count. To assess the practical relevance a more thorough investigation is needed.

Conclusion

The first steps are made to study the WeRateDogs phenomenon. Over time the ratings increase. Tweets are more frequent on weekdays, from which on Monday we expect most Tweets. Pictures identified as dogs by the algorithm are not clearly correlated to how much humans like the picture.

The cleaned dataset used for the analysis is available in CSV format in the download section below. Interesting questions are not yet tackled.
